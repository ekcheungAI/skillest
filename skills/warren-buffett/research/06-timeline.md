## Era 1: Origins — The Omaha Boy (1930-1956)

| | |
|---|---|
| **Age** | 0 → 26 |
| **Years** | 1930-1956 |
| **Role** | Son, Student, Entrepreneur, protégé |
| **Defining event** | Meeting Benjamin Graham, Columbia Business School |
| **Behavioral signature** | Extreme financial precocity; every dollar was a capital allocation experiment |

### Key Events

- **1930**: Born August 30, Omaha, Nebraska. Father Howard was a stockbroker who later became Congressman.
- **1941**: At 11, bought first stock (Cities Service Preferred, $6,318 profit). Filed first income tax return at 14. The seed of long-term orientation was planted early.
- **1945**: Ran a pinball machine business with a friend. Bought one machine for $25, grew to 7 machines, sold the route for $1,200. First lesson in scaling and exit.
- **1947-1950**: Wharton (University of Pennsylvania), then back to Nebraska. Read "The Intelligent Investor" by Benjamin Graham at 19. Life changed.
- **1950**: Applied to Harvard Business School. Rejected. Went to Columbia instead — where Graham taught. The most consequential rejection in investment history.
- **1951**: First investments. Bought GEICO ($10,282, lifetime return >$1M). Met Charlie Munger.
- **1954**: Joined Graham-Newman Partnership as analyst.
- **1956**: Graham retired. Buffett returned to Omaha with $174,000 (~$1M in today's money) and started Buffett Associates Ltd.

### Behavioral Signature: Extreme Rationality in a Materialistic Family

Howard Buffett was absent (politics), mother was warm. Warren learned: don't depend emotionally on people; depend on rational calculation. This is the origin of the emotional detachment that defines his investment style.

## Era 2: Partnership Years — The Compounder (1956-1969)

| | |
|---|---|
| **Age** | 26 → 39 |
| **Years** | 1956-1969 |
| **Role** | General Partner, Investment Fund Manager |
| **Defining event** | 31.6% annual returns, closing the partnership in 1969 |
| **Behavioral signature** | Asymmetric fee structures; no losing years; radical transparency in partnership letters |

### Key Events

- **1956**: Buffett Associates Ltd. formed. Seven family members and friends invested $105,000. Buffett put $5,000 of his own money. Fee: 0% management fee + 25% of profits above 6% hurdle.
- **1957-1968**: Earned 31.6% compounded annually for LPs. No losing years. The Dow returned 9.1% in the same period. $10,000 invested with Buffett in 1957 → $294,000 in 1969.
- **1957-1962**: Cigar butt investments dominated. Found cheap businesses others overlooked. Net-net stocks. Distressed securities.
- **1959**: First met Charlie Munger in Omaha. Their partnership was forged over decades, not days.
- **1961**: American Express (after Salad Oil scandal). First "wonderful business" purchase — paid premium for quality.
- **1962**: First Berkshire Hathaway shares purchased (~$7.50/share). A textile company that became his permanent vehicle.
- **1965**: Took control of Berkshire Hathaway. Discovered he was emotionally trapped — couldn't liquidate because minority partners would have been hurt.
- **1969**: Closed the partnership. Gave partners choice of: cash, Bartlett & Co., or Sequoia Fund. This was the greatest act of integrity in Buffett's career — giving LPs their money back rather than continuing to compound at lower rates in a richer market.
- **1970**: First Berkshire Hathaway annual letter.

### Behavioral Signature: The Fee Structure as Identity

The 0/6/25 structure (0% management fee, 25% of losses, 50% of profits above 6%) made Buffett rich only when LPs got rich first. This asymmetric structure was his competitive advantage in fundraising — and it shaped his reputation as someone whose interests were aligned with (not above) his investors.

## Era 3: Berkshire Accumulation — The Fortress Builder (1970-1999)

| | |
|---|---|
| **Age** | 39 → 69 |
| **Years** | 1970-1999 |
| **Role** | Chairman & CEO, Berkshire Hathaway |
| **Defining event** | National Indemnity (1967, but profits grew through this era); Charlie Munger becomes Vice Chairman |
| **Behavioral signature** | Float-powered capital allocation; decentralization; quality over price shift; the "wide moat" framework emerges |

### Key Events

- **1970s**: The pivot. Charlie Munger systematically challenged Buffett's Graham-style cigar butt approach. "A wonderful business at a fair price beats a fair business at a wonderful price."
- **1972**: See's Candies. Paid $25M. Still generating profits today. The lesson: brand moats are durable.
- **1976**: GEICO turnaround. Invested $45M when GEICO was near bankruptcy. Lou Simpson ran it back to profitability. By 1996, fully acquired GEICO for $2.3B.
- **1987**: Salomon Preferred. Buffett became interim chairman after a treasury fraud scandal. The one time he said "I trusted the wrong person."
- **1987**: October 19 crash. BRK held mostly cash. Didn't buy aggressively — waited for clarity.
- **1988**: Coca-Cola. $1.3B investment in what became the most successful public equity investment in Berkshire history. "Best decision I ever made."
- **1996**: Fully acquired GEICO.
- **1998**: General Re. $22B in stock. "The end of my first era." Paid too much in stock.
- **1999**: MidAmerican Energy. $9.3B. "I should have bought it 10 years earlier."

### The Charlie Munger Influence (Era 3's defining theme)

This era is the story of Buffett's thinking evolving from Graham's cigar butt to Munger's quality moats. The shift happened slowly — Buffett still called See's a "cigar butt that happened to keep smoking" — but the direction was clear by the late 1980s.

**Key Munger quote:**
> "Warren learned from me that the best businesses are those with wide, durable moats that give the owner pricing power for decades."

**Buffett's response (with characteristic self-deprecation):**
> "Charlie taught me that I should look for wonderful businesses and hold them forever. I've been slow to learn, but I did eventually learn."

### Behavioral Signature: The Float Flywheel

The Berkshire insurance model emerged fully in this era: National Indemnity + GEICO generated float → float invested at Berkshire's cost of capital ($0) → returns compounded tax-deferred → more insurance float generated → repeat. This was the structural competitive advantage that no other investor could replicate.

## Era 4: Global Fame — The Oracle's Vindication (1999-2008)

| | |
|---|---|
| **Age** | 69 → 78 |
| **Years** | 1999-2008 |
| **Role** | Oracle of Omaha |
| **Defining event** | The Carnival speech; Dot-com crash vindication; Goldman Sachs investment |
| **Behavioral signature** | Explicit contrarianism; "Quiet! I am thinking"; willingness to wait years while being called irrelevant |

### Key Events

- **1999**: Fortune article + annual meeting: "The 'Carnival' speech." Buffett explicitly warned about internet valuations. He was called old, out-of-touch. He was right.
- **2000**: Nasdaq peaked March 10. Began 3-year decline of 78%.
- **2001**: September 11. Market closed. Reopened. Buffett appeared on CNBC saying "Buy American. I am."
- **2003**: Munger named Vice Chairman (formally). Decades of de facto partnership made official.
- **2005**: Dan Loeb letter. The "Buffett hypocrisy" debate about buybacks. This is the only sustained public criticism Buffett has faced.
- **2007**: "I will continue to invest in American businesses." S&P down 50% from peak.
- **2008**: Goldman Sachs $5B preferred. "I'm buying when there's blood in the streets." Return: >$3.7B on the preferred alone, plus warrants.

### The Carnival Speech — Defining Moment of Era 4

Buffett was universally mocked in 1999. He was called "a relic." His response at the annual meeting:

> "Quiet! I am thinking."

Three words that defined an era. He knew he was right. He was willing to wait. And he was right.

### Behavioral Signature: Crisis Alpha in Practice

Era 4 proved that Buffett's "patient capital" wasn't passive — it was actively deployed at extremes. 2008-2009 was the largest capital deployment period in Berkshire history (relative to market conditions). Goldman Sachs, Swiss Re, various others — all at crisis prices with structural protections.

## Era 5: Succession & Transition — The Long Goodbye (2009-2026)

| | |
|---|---|
| **Age** | 78 → 95 |
| **Years** | 2009-2026 |
| **Role** | Aging Oracle; Succession Architect |
| **Defining event** | Apple investment (2016); Apple selling (2023-2024); Greg Abel transition; record cash position |
| **Behavioral signature** | Continued discipline + willingness to evolve (Apple); systematic Apple selling; massive buybacks; succession clarity |

### Key Events

- **2009-2012**: Post-crisis recovery. Burlington Northern Santa Fe ($44B, largest acquisition).
- **2015**: Kraft Heinz. Buffett called it "a rare blunder." Debt + market share erosion = ongoing loss.
- **2016**: Apple first purchase. $1B initial investment → grew to $62B peak.
- **2018**: Succession announcement: Greg Abel will be CEO when Buffett departs. Ajit Jain will run insurance.
- **2020**: COVID crash. Unusual restraint — Berkshire didn't buy aggressively during the March 2020 lows. Held record cash ($146B).
- **2020**: "Never bet against America." Pandemic reassurance letter.
- **2022**: Charlie Munger died (November 28). Buffett: "60% of whatever I've done is due to Charlie."
- **2023**: Began selling Apple. Sold ~$115B total over 2023-2024.
- **2024**: Berkshire buybacks continued. Cash position remained elevated ($168B+).
- **2025-2026**: Abel taking more public prominence. Buffett increasingly hands questions to Abel.

### The Apple Decision — Era 5's Central Drama

Buffett bought Apple NOT as a tech company but as a "consumer franchise with a pricing moat." He studied how iPhone users behave. He decided switching costs made it a durable business.

When he started selling Apple, he justified it as "valuation discipline." The market called it "selling the best investment ever."

**The paradox:** Selling Apple is consistent with the Peak Sell Protocol. It is NOT consistent with the "forever holding" rhetoric. Buffett has never fully resolved this publicly — he deflects with "valuation, not sentiment."

### Behavioral Signature: Succession Without Surrender

Buffett has managed succession planning for 20+ years. He's been explicit about it. He's groomed Abel publicly. But he's not leaving. At 95 (2026), he remains Chairman and CEO.

**What changed under Abel:** More buybacks, faster portfolio adjustment, willingness to sell Apple more aggressively.
