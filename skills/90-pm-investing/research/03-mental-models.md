# 90-pm-investing — Mental Models & Framework Deep-Dive

## The Five Intellectual Influences

### 1. Peter Lynch — Industry Knowledge as Alpha Source

**Contribution:** Lynch's core insight — "invest in what you know" — is not about buying what you personally consume. It's about recognizing that your professional domain expertise gives you access to information that analysts lack.

**Key Teachings (as cited by 90PM):**
- "My number one rule is: don't listen to the professionals. With just 3% of the brain you use in ordinary life, anyone can pick stocks as well as Wall Street experts — often better."
- "Buy what you know" means: use your industry experience to generate *hypotheses*, not to form *convictions*. Your experience tells you where to look — the framework tells you how to verify.
- Lynch earned his best returns in years 3-4 of holdings — institutional short-term review cycles would have forced exit earlier.
- Institutional constraints: funds must be diversified beyond utility, reducing any single position's impact.

**90PM's Adaptation:**
- Industry knowledge → hypothesis generation (not conviction)
- Pattern recognition → falsifiable question framing
- Patient compounding → disciplined kill condition tracking

### 2. Charlie Munger — Mental Models Latticework

**Contribution:** Munger argues that you need ~100 mental models from major disciplines — physics, psychology, economics, mathematics — and that the best investors see reality more accurately because they have a more complete latticework of frameworks.

**Key Teachings (as cited by 90PM):**
- Mental models across disciplines prevent single-domain blind spots
- Inversion: "Show me where someone will die, so I can avoid that place"
- Seeking best available alternatives: opportunity cost thinking

**90PM's Adaptation:**
- Mental models → MECE tree decomposition
- Cross-disciplinary thinking → Porter's Five Forces, S-Curve, Brand Pyramid applied to equity analysis
- Latticework → "what X vs what Non-X" brainstorm dimension selection

### 3. Richard Feynman — Scientific Falsification

**Contribution:** The most central intellectual influence. Feynman's methodological approach to science — try to prove yourself wrong as quickly as possible — is directly imported into investment thesis validation.

**Key Teachings:**

**The Four-Step Scientific Method (Cornell lecture):**
1. **Guess** — formulate a hypothesis
2. **Compute** — derive predictions from the hypothesis
3. **Compare** — compare predictions to experimental data
4. **Conclude** — if comparison fails, hypothesis is wrong (no exceptions)

**Feynman's Key Quote:**
> "Scientists always try to prove the rules are wrong. That is exactly what you must do. ... The scientist is never certain of his rules. ... He knows all rules are wrong, and yet he follows them anyway, hoping that for the particular problem he is working on, the error is not fatal."

**Feynman's Challenge Response (1963 Cornell "Uncertainty of Science" lecture):**
> Q: "How can you live in a state of never knowing?"
> A: "I don't know what you mean. I always live in that state. It's easy. What I don't know is far more interesting than what I do know."

**The "Keep a Crack in the Door" Principle:**
> "I think that the way to do it is to realize that the most important ideas will be ones which you are not certain of — that you would be able to be wrong. Otherwise, if you're all set and determined that you know the right answer, you will never find out anything new."

**90PM's Direct Import:**
- "什麼情況出現了，我承認自己是錯的" = Feynman's falsification condition
- Kill condition = experimental exit criteria
- Quarterly earnings = experimental data
- Leaf node conclusion = experimental result (🟢🟡🔴)

## The Core Framework: Narrative-First Analysis

### The World is a Web

The foundational insight: reality is a tangled web of interdependencies. Everything affects everything else — interest rates, valuation, sentiment, liquidity, competition, policy, industry cycles. There is no single starting point and no linear causation.

**Problem:** You cannot operate on a web. You need structure.

**Solution:** Decompose the web into a tree (MECE).

**Output:** Convert the tree back into linear text for communication.

This is Steven Pinker's insight, directly quoted by 90PM: *"The world is a web, structure is a tree, text is linear."*

## The Five-Signal Framework

Before forming a thesis, detect the market's current narrative through five signal sources:

### Signal 1: Valuation Metric Selection

The metric analysts choose reveals the story they believe.

| Metric | Implied Story | Example |
|--------|---------------|---------|
| P/E | Mature, stable, predictable earnings | Mature industrial |
| EV/Revenue | Growth story, profitability not yet relevant | High-growth SaaS |
| P/B | Cyclical or asset-heavy | Banks, miners |
| EV/EBITDA | Cash flow story | Leveraged buyouts |
| NAV/SOTP | Sum-of-parts; hidden assets | Conglomerates |
| DCF | Long-duration, uncertain cash flows | Pre-profit biotech |

**Key insight:** When analysts split on metric (some use P/E, others use EV/Revenue), the market is in narrative transition — the highest-value window.

### Signal 2: Peer Group Mapping

Who analysts compare a company to reveals its identity in the market's mind.

**Ferrari Example:**
- Peer = Toyota/BMW/GM → Story: "Automotive manufacturer" → P/E ceiling 8-15x
- Peer = Hermès/LVMH → Story: "Luxury brand" → P/E ceiling 30-50x

**NVDA Example:**
- Peer = AMD/INTC/TXN → Story: "Semiconductor cycle" → P/E ceiling 20-30x
- Peer = MSFT/GOOGL/ORCL → Story: "AI infrastructure platform" → P/E ceiling 40-60x

**Key insight:** Peer drift over 6-12 months signals narrative re-set in progress.

### Signal 3: Earnings Call Q&A Themes

Analyst questions reveal what the market considers most uncertain.

| Question Type | Market Concern | Story Phase |
|--------------|---------------|-------------|
| "AI revenue %? AI pipeline?" | Growth thesis validation | Growth story |
| "Can margins hold?" | Profitability sustainability | Transition to maturity |
| "Any buyback plans?" | Capital allocation; maturity signal | Cash cow phase |
| "Competitive win rates?" | Moat integrity | Concern phase |

**Key insight:** Track themes across 2-3 consecutive calls — the direction of questioning shift predicts narrative evolution.

### Signal 4: Fund Flows and Positioning

Where money is flowing reveals conviction, not just opinion.

- **ETF classification** → Passive money mechanically follows index rules
- **Institutional positioning** → Growth vs value drift
- **Flow direction** → Real-time conviction signals

**Key insight:** ETF inclusion/exclusion mechanically moves prices regardless of fundamentals. Passive = largest holder in many stocks.

### Signal 5: One-Line Media Tag

The compressed narrative — how the story survives in a retweet.

Examples:
- "AI picks and shovels" → Infrastructure vs applications framing
- "Oil is dead" → Permanent structural decline narrative
- "China uninvestable" → Country risk contagion narrative
- "Growth is peaking" → Lifecycle transition narrative
- "Cash burning machine" → Unsustainable business model

**Key insight:** Watch for permanent language — "forever", "結構性", "不可逆" — as alarm triggers. Markets systematically misprice "permanent" narratives.

## Six Types of Narrative Errors

### Error 1: Identity Error
**Definition:** Market assigns wrong category → wrong peer → wrong multiple.

**Mechanism:** Ferrari (luxury goods) compared to Toyota (auto manufacturer) → same sector, vastly different valuation ceiling.

**90PM's NVDA Thesis:** Market treats NVDA as a semiconductor cycle stock → compares to INTC/AMD → P/E ceiling ~20-30x. But NVDA is actually an AI infrastructure monopoly → should compare to MSFT/GOOGL → P/E ceiling 40-60x.

### Error 2: Permanence Error
**Definition:** Temporary treated as permanent, or vice versa.

**Mechanism:** Pandemic oil demand collapse treated as structural long-term shift → "oil is dead." (OXY trade thesis.)

**2021 Growth Stocks:** Temporary pandemic acceleration treated as permanent structural change → SaaS stocks priced at 30-50x revenue → growth normalized → stocks fell 60-80%.

### Error 3: Lifecycle Mismatch
**Definition:** Company priced in wrong lifecycle stage.

**Mechanism:** Mature company still priced with growth multiples (growth premium) OR growth company prematurely re-rated as mature.

**Tech Lifecycle Compression:** Tech company lifecycles are compressed — rise faster, mature sooner, decline steeper. Using traditional industry timing frameworks on tech systematically misprices it.

### Error 4: Narrative Inertia
**Definition:** Business has fundamentally changed but story stays in the old version.

**Facebook 2012:** Mobile ad revenue growing from 0% → 62% of total. Analysts still writing reports asking "can Facebook monetize mobile?" years after data answered the question.

**Microsoft 2014-2016:** Nadella pivoting to cloud. Market still pricing as "Windows licensing company" → ~14x P/E for 2 years while cloud transition progressed.

**Mechanism:** GICS classifications, analyst coverage groups, ETF inclusions have structural inertia. Business changes but categorization doesn't update automatically.

### Error 5: Discrete Event Pricing
**Definition:** Binary all-or-nothing pricing instead of probability-weighted expected value.

**Mechanism:** Humans are bad at probability estimation. Markets tend to price discrete events (M&A, FDA approvals, regulatory decisions) as 0% or 100% rather than 60% / 40%.

**Examples:**
- M&A premium priced at 95% certainty → deal breaks → stock falls back to pre-announcement price
- FDA approval priced as certain → rejection → 50% crash
- China delisting risk priced as 5% → risk jumps to 50% → 50% P/E compression across sector

### Error 6: Macro Contagion
**Definition:** Sector narrative suppresses individual companies regardless of fundamentals.

**Mechanism:** ETF flows and passive investing mechanically move stocks by sector, not by individual company merit. Macro narrative acts as a virus spreading through classification systems.

**Examples:**
- 2019-2020 "Oil is dead": All oil stocks sold indiscriminately regardless of balance sheet quality
- 2021-2022 "China uninvestable": Entire ADRs priced as if forced delisting certain → included fundamentally healthy companies
- 2023-2025 "AI changes everything": Any company with AI connection priced as if AI revenue is 100% of business

## Three Tree Drawing Methods

### Method 1: Formula (Best for: High-Information Companies with Clear Financial Identity)

**Logic:** A financial formula is naturally exhaustive. Revenue = A × B × C → every dollar of revenue must come from one of these variables.

**Example — Meta:**
```
Ad Revenue = DAP × Time × Ad Load × Yield
```

Six branches (3 revenue drivers + 3 cost drivers) → 24 leaf nodes total.

**Advantage:** Automatic completeness. You cannot accidentally miss a variable because the formula is an identity.

### Method 2: Process (Best for: Industrial, Infrastructure, Companies with Sequential Milestones)

**Logic:** Business success depends on events occurring in sequence. Each milestone is a branch.

**Example — EOSE:**
```
Demand → Technology → Scale-up → Shareholder Return
```

**Warning:** Process methods may miss parallel paths or cross-cutting risks. Add a "management integrity" branch for fraud risk.

### Method 3: X vs Non-X (Best for: Commodities, Supply/Demand Markets, MECE Clarity)

**Logic:** Every set can be divided into X and Non-X. This is mathematically MECE by definition.

**Example — OXY (Oil):**
```
Oil Price = Supply × Demand
Supply = Existing Supply + New Supply
Demand = Short-term + Long-term
```

**Advantage:** Impossible to accidentally miss a category — X + Non-X = 100% by definition.

## The Science/Art Divide

**Science (90PM's domain):**
- Tree drawing
- MECE decomposition
- Falsifiable hypothesis formation
- Kill condition setting
- Data tracking

**Art (remains with the investor):**
- Position sizing given probability distribution
- Risk tolerance calibration
- Time horizon alignment with life circumstances
- Conviction level vs confidence level

**Howard Marks' framing:**
> "We may never know where we're going, but we'd better have a good idea where we are."

**The tree tells you where you are. It doesn't tell you how much to bet.**
