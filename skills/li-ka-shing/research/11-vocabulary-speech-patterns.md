# 11 — Vocabulary & Speech Patterns

## Why This Chapter Matters

The Duck Principle (鸭子哲学) in `SKILL.md` states: Li Ka-shing's public communication reveals almost nothing of actual intent. This chapter is the empirical grounding for that claim — an analysis of the specific vocabulary, rhetorical structures, and code-switching patterns that define Li Ka-shing's speech across different registers.

**Source:** All four Shantou University commencement speeches (2013, 2014, 2017, 2018); Forbes 2006 interview; Bloomberg 2016 interview; Medium/Global Times Singapore (2024); Shantou 2017 full text via Firecrawl (2026-04-10); Foundation website.

---

## The Two Registers

Li Ka-shing operates in **two completely distinct communication registers**:

### Register 1: Public / Ceremonial (English)
**Used in:** Commencement speeches, press releases, ESG messages, formal interviews
**Audience:** International media, academics, graduates, general public
**Goal:** Inspire, moralize, establish values — reveal nothing specific

### Register 2: Internal / Operational (Cantonese / Mandarin)
**Used in:** Board meetings, private conversations with executives, deal negotiations
**Audience:** Inner circle only
**Goal:** Specific, numerate, direct — no platitudes, no ambiguity
**Note:** This register is never publicly documented. The characteristics below are inferred from third-party accounts (executives who have worked with him, Bloomberg reporting, Medium biography).

### The "Water" Metaphor

Li frequently uses water imagery — understood in the Cantonese business community as a direct reference to *道家* (Taoist) philosophy absorbed through Chaozhou/Shantou regional culture:

> "Be water" — adaptation of Bruce Lee's famous formulation

**Application:** Li Ka-shing describes adaptability, fluidity, and non-resistance as core business virtues. The metaphor is strategically vague in public (it sounds like a general life philosophy) but in internal communication it translates to: **"Find the path of least resistance to your goal. Do not force confrontation when a negotiated path exists."**

This is the Duck Principle in metaphor form.

### The "Goose" Metaphor (金蛋鹅)

> "The goose that lays the golden eggs" — appears in interviews, speeches, and is the most cited Li Ka-shing investment concept

**Public framing:** A general analogy about nurturing assets and avoiding short-termism.
**Operational meaning (internal):** A specific, testable framework for every acquisition decision (see `03-the-goose-test.md`).

The gap between the public metaphor and the internal framework is the entire Duck Principle made visible.

### Rhetorical Structure of Public Speeches

**Pattern identified across all 4 Shantou speeches:**

1. **Opening Hook:** Personal anecdote or vulnerability disclosure (surgery 2013; early hardship 2014; AI transformation 2017; final speech 2018)
2. **Broad Problem Statement:** Social challenge or generational concern (inequality, technology disruption, moral decline)
3. **Moral Call to Action:** Abstract principle derived from classical Chinese or Western literature (Mencius, WB Yeats, Napoleon, Wang Yangming)
4. **Personal Commitment:** Li Ka-shing's own example as illustration (Shantou University, Foundation giving)
5. **Forward-Looking Charge:** Graduates as agents of change; future as collective responsibility

**Zero specifics in every speech.** No policy proposals. No named investments. No political positions. No business targets.

## Register 1 — Cantonese / Mandarin (Internal, Inferred)

This register is reconstructed from third-party accounts (executives, Medium biography, Bloomberg):

### Characteristics:

| Trait | Manifestation |
|---|---|
| **Direct** | No hedging language; decisions stated as facts |
| **Numerate** | Specific multiples, percentages, deal valuations — no rounding |
| **Time-specific** | "Hold this for 10 years, not 5" — explicit holding period |
| **Risk-binary** | "If Beijing can block this, we don't do it" — clear kill conditions |
| **Minimal pleasantries** | Efficiency over relationship maintenance; Cantonese business norm |
| **Silence as signal** | Extended pauses; if Li doesn't respond for 30+ seconds, the proposal is effectively rejected |
| **The "No" that isn't "No"** | Li rarely says no directly. Instead: "Think about it more carefully" or "The timing isn't right" or silence. |

### The Cantonese Code-Switch

The single most important linguistic pattern for understanding Li Ka-shing's operational communication:

**In English (public):** "We are committed to the long-term sustainability of our businesses."
**In Cantonese (private, inferred):** "This asset either passes the four questions or it doesn't."

The shift from vague commitment language (English) to testable framework language (Cantonese) is not accidental — it is the **structural encoding of the Duck Principle in language itself.**

## Vocabulary in Context: The Shantou 2013 Speech (Full Analysis)

From the speech text retrieved via Firecrawl (2026-04-10):

**Opening vulnerability disclosure:**
> "I recently suffered acute cholecystitis and underwent a gallbladder removal surgery... True to form, I tried to be 'productive' during my few days of 'sick leave'."

**Analysis:** Even personal medical events are framed as productivity opportunities. The self-discipline narrative is consistent across all public appearances.

**The "no service zones" metaphor:**
> "Is your spectrum of empathy so filled with 'no service zones' that you cannot pick up the sighs and signals of helplessness?"

**Analysis:** Li uses telecommunications imagery (cellular "no service zones") — from a man who built a telecom empire. This is **domain-specific metaphor layering**: the audience (Shantou students, many in engineering/tech) hears the telecom metaphor; it reinforces Li's identity as both educator and industry titan simultaneously.

**The small finger scar:**
> "This little finger bore my first scar... I saw through the window 'elders' clustering near the heater and savoring a lovely cup of hot tea. Distracted by a sudden rush of bitterness, forlornness and loneliness."

**Analysis:** This is the only direct emotional disclosure in any public speech. Li allows a moment of raw vulnerability — the 14-year-old boy at the plastics factory — precisely because it is **so specific** that it paradoxically feels more honest than platitudes, without revealing anything about current business strategy.

## The Forbes 2006 Interview: Vocabulary Precision

> "I have made a profit every year since 1950. I have never lost a penny in any year."

This is the most numerically precise statement Li Ka-shing has ever made publicly. 56 consecutive profitable years, stated as a fact.

**Why this matters:** This is the only public statement where Li uses **extreme numerical specificity** to support a business claim. It is not an accident that he chose this interview (Forbes, US business audience) to make this statement. The precision signals: *I am not a storyteller, I am a record-keeper. My track record speaks for itself.*

In the Cantonese register, this would translate to: "I pass my own four questions. That's the only standard that matters."

## Vocabulary Pattern: The Anti-Brag

Li Ka-shing's public statements consistently avoid:
- Naming competitors
- Celebrating specific deals or exits
- Criticizing governments, regulators, or political conditions
- Making numerical predictions (revenue, profit, market share)
- Using dramatic or superlative language about himself

This restraint is consistent across **all 70 years of documented communication.** It is not a personality trait — it is a **systematic communication strategy** for maintaining maximum political neutrality.

## Applied: How to Use This Chapter in Coaching

When coaching a student on a decision:

**If the student is being vague:** Redirect to Li's Cantonese register: "Li Ka-shing would ask: what is the specific kill scenario, the exact holding period, the numerical return threshold. Not 'it's a good long-term bet.'"

**If the student is being too public:** Remind them of the Anti-Brag principle: "The most powerful statements are made in specific private language, not public generalities."

**If the student confuses metaphors with frameworks:** Point to the Goose Test: "The public metaphor is the tip of the iceberg. The operational framework is beneath the waterline. Always ask: what does this metaphor mean in specific, testable terms?"
