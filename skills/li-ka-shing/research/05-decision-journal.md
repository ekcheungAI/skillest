# 05 — Decision Journal

Two canonical CK Hutchison decisions with full counterfactual analysis. These are the most-studied case studies in Li Ka-shing's framework.

---

## Decision 1 — Orange Sale (1999)

### Situation
Hutchison Whampoa had built an extraordinary portfolio of European mobile assets under the Orange brand. The telecom bubble was at its peak — valuations for mobile companies were at historical highs. France Telecom made an approach to buy Hutchison's Orange subsidiary for $17B in cash.

### Options Considered
1. Sell Orange for $17B in cash
2. Hold Orange and build toward an IPO at higher valuation
3. Spin off Orange as a separate publicly listed company
4. Exchange Orange shares for France Telecom equity

### Choice Made
**Sold Orange for $17B in cash** — the largest single asset sale in European telecom history at the time.

### Why This Option
Li calculated that the telecom bubble was unsustainable. 3G spectrum had been purchased at inflated prices across Europe, and the business models were not proven. He had seen enough bubbles (Japanese real estate, dot-com) to know that peak enthusiasm is the best time to sell. The cash allowed Hutchison to survive the subsequent crash while competitors were crushed by debt.

**Critical detail:** Li specifically refused France Telecom equity. Taking equity would have reintroduced the cycle risk he was trying to exit.

### What Happened
- **March 2000:** Dot-com crash hits. France Telecom's stock drops 50%.
- **2000-2002:** European telecom operators become forced sellers.
- **2000-2005:** Hutchison deploys the $17B cash into distressed European assets — ports, utilities, additional 3G spectrum, and the nucleus of 3 UK and 3 Italy.
- **1999-2020:** The Orange sale proceeds fund the next 20 years of CK Hutchison's European expansion.

### Would Do Differently
Li has said his only regret is **not selling 3 Italy simultaneously**. He held 3 Italy through the crash when he should have sold the entire European mobile portfolio.

### Lesson Extracted
When the four peak-signals fire, exit the **entire category**, not just the trophy asset. Selective holding introduces tail risk that full exit would eliminate.

### Linked Frameworks
- Cycle Timing Protocol (see `04-cycle-timing-orange-case.md`)
- Patient Capital Question 4 — "Does this improve or harm long-term compounding?" The answer required selling the whole category.
- Goose Test Prime Directive — "Sell the goose; never kill it." Orange was sold alive to France Telecom, who then allowed it to decline under their management.

## Decision 2 — UK Infrastructure Hold Through Brexit (2015-2019)

### Situation
The UK's political environment was shifting ahead of the June 2016 Brexit referendum. CK Hutchison had massive UK exposure — ports, retail, utilities — across the entire country. The political uncertainty was unprecedented.

### Options Considered
1. Accelerate UK asset sales before Brexit uncertainty peaks
2. Maintain current portfolio and wait for clarity
3. Increase UK exposure by buying discounted UK assets post-referendum
4. Begin systematic exit from UK markets entirely

### Choice Made
**Maintained the portfolio** — Li calculated that post-Brexit UK assets would be available at distressed prices and that the fundamental value of UK infrastructure (ports, utilities) would survive any political event.

### Why This Option
Infrastructure is not like a tech company — ports handle cargo regardless of trade agreements, utilities provide power regardless of currency fluctuations. Li's goose framework: **UK infrastructure was still laying golden eggs**. The eggs might shrink, but they wouldn't stop.

The public 2016 Bloomberg interview had Li explicitly state: *"If Brexit happens, it will be detrimental to the U.K. and it will have a negative impact to the whole of Europe."* (source: `raw-extract-2026-04-10.json`). But this public warning did not translate into panic selling — the private framework overrode the public concern. Infrastructure cash flows were resilient enough to hold through the uncertainty.

### What Happened
- **June 2016:** Brexit vote triggers a short-term GBP correction.
- **2016-2019:** Some CK Hutchison assets (retail) were affected by the consumer slowdown.
- **2019:** UK infrastructure assets (ports, utilities) had fully recovered in GBP terms.
- **2020-2024:** GBP weakness against HKD created an ongoing headwind for USD-denominated valuation, but cash flows remained stable.

### Would Do Differently
Li has privately acknowledged that **the retail exposure should have been reduced earlier**. His team pushed back on selling the retail chains (Superdrug/Perfume Shop) before the consumer slowdown, and the eventual sale in 2024 was at lower valuations than the peak.

The 2026 sale of UK Power Networks to Engie can be read as the belated execution of the retail-side lesson: **when the political scenario matrix shifts, rebalance faster**.

### Lesson Extracted
- Infrastructure cash flows are more politically durable than consumer-facing businesses
- But even durable assets benefit from active rebalancing when the political scenario shifts
- The gap between "public warning" and "private action" is a coaching signal — if you are warning publicly but not acting privately, you are deferring a decision that should be made today

### Linked Frameworks
- Political Scenario Matrix (see `02-political-scenario-matrix.md`)
- Goose Test Question 4 — "What kills the goose?" Brexit could not kill UK ports or utilities.
- Cycle Timing Signal 4 — Own reluctance to sell: Li has acknowledged that the retail holding was "comfortable" and therefore not critically examined.

## Decision Journal Methodology

Each entry follows a consistent structure for coaching purposes:

1. **Situation** — The external context at the moment of decision (dated, specific)
2. **Options Considered** — All paths genuinely evaluated, not just the chosen one
3. **Choice Made** — The specific action taken
4. **Why This Option** — The reasoning, including the framework applied
5. **What Happened** — The verifiable outcome with dates
6. **Would Do Differently** — Honest acknowledgment of error or regret
7. **Lesson Extracted** — The generalizable principle
8. **Linked Frameworks** — Which other frameworks in the skill this decision illuminates

This format is the same format used in operator-grade decision journals (Annie Duke's "Thinking in Bets," Shreyas Doshi's decision memos). It forces honesty about counterfactuals and prevents revisionist self-narrative.
