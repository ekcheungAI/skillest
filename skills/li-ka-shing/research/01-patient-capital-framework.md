# 01 — The Patient Capital Framework

## Origin

Li Ka-shing did not invent this framework in a single moment. It crystallized over seven decades, built from specific pivotal experiences:

- **1940s (Refugee in Hong Kong)** — His father died of TB. Li left school at 15 to work in a plastics factory. The foundational lesson: **capital preservation is survival**, not strategy.
- **1950s (Cheung Kong Plastics)** — Building a factory from nothing. The compounding discovery: small daily reinvestment into equipment, over 10 years, builds a moat that no large competitor can catch.
- **1967 Hong Kong riots** — Political panic crashed real estate. Li bought. This was the first deliberate application of what would become the cycle timing protocol.
- **1979 Hutchison acquisition** — The first Chinese to acquire a British-controlled Hong Kong conglomerate. Taught him that **political goodwill is the enabler of every major transaction**.

## The Four Questions (in Strict Order)

### Question 1: What could permanently impair this capital?

**Not "what could go wrong" — that's infinite.** Specifically: what event, regulatory action, political shift, or structural change would make this capital unrecoverable?

- If the answer involves a single government's decision → the position is too exposed. Diversify before entering, or pass.
- If the answer is "nothing short of global collapse" → proceed to Question 2.

**Worked example (UK infrastructure, 2000-2020):** Before buying UK Power Networks, the question was: what could permanently impair this asset? Answer: nationalization under a hard-left Labour government. Probability: low but non-zero. Mitigation: structure the holding through Canadian and Dutch entities to maintain international arbitration protection. Only after this structural protection was in place did Li approve the acquisition.

### Question 2: Is the return adequate for the risk?

Patient capital demands a premium over risk-free rates that compounds meaningfully over decades. The math matters:

| Annual return | 30-year multiple | 50-year multiple |
|---|---|---|
| 4% | 3.24x | 7.11x |
| 6% | 5.74x | 18.42x |
| 8% | 10.06x | 46.90x |
| 10% | 17.45x | 117.39x |
| 12% | 29.96x | 289.00x |

A 2-percentage-point gap over 50 years is a **6.4x difference in terminal wealth**. This is why Li's compounding floor is non-negotiable — the difference is not linear, it is the entire game.

### Question 3: Can I hold this through a 10-year political cycle?

Can I still own this asset under:
- A different government?
- A different trade regime?
- A different currency regime?
- A different regulatory environment?

If holding requires a **specific political condition** to persist, the asset is not an investment — it is a bet on that political condition.

**Worked example (Canadian utilities, 1987-present):** Li's purchase of Husky Oil (later Husky Energy) in 1987 was sized to survive any Canadian government. It has been held continuously through 7 Prime Ministers, three major regulatory overhauls, and two oil price crashes. The 10-year political cycle test was passed continuously for 38 years.

### Question 4: Does this improve long-term compounding, or does it harm it?

Some investments look attractive standalone but damage the system:
- They consume disproportionate management attention
- They use political goodwill that is needed elsewhere
- They introduce correlated risk that reduces portfolio stability
- They require capital that would compound better deployed in existing assets

**Worked example (rejected tech venture bets):** Li has repeatedly declined to make direct investments in Chinese tech startups despite having the capital and the access. The stated reason: he cannot verify technology claims and would have to rely on advisors with potentially biased interests. The unstated reason: even a successful tech bet would consume political goodwill in Beijing at a moment when CK Hutchison's overall China exposure needed reduction, not expansion. Question 4 killed the investment category entirely.

## The Drill

Take any investment in your portfolio. Run the four questions:

1. Answer Q1 with a specific kill scenario (not "market risk").
2. Answer Q2 with a specific compounding number (not "decent returns").
3. Answer Q3 with a specific 10-year holding confirmation under 3+ political scenarios.
4. Answer Q4 with a portfolio-level impact assessment (not "it's a good deal").

**If you cannot answer all four with specifics, you do not own the investment — you are gambling on it.**

## What This Framework Does NOT Cover

- Short-horizon trading (sub-3 years)
- Technology startups where the founder cannot verify claims
- Market-timing specific political events
- Assets requiring leverage above 50% to generate adequate returns

For these decisions, a different framework is required. The honest answer in coaching: **redirect**.
