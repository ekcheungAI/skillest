## Full Biography

Peter Lynch (1944–present) is one of the most successful investment managers in history. As portfolio manager of Fidelity's Magellan Fund from 1977 to 1990, he achieved a 29.2% average annual return, outperforming the S&P 500 by approximately 13% per year for 13 consecutive years. Lynch's approach was fundamentally different from Simons' quantitative methods: he relied on deep fundamental research, personal investigation, and unrelenting focus on company quality. He famously visited hundreds of companies annually to understand their businesses firsthand. Lynch's investment philosophy was transparent and teachable—he believed average investors could beat professionals by focusing on companies they understand, maintaining long-term perspective, and avoiding emotional decision-making. After retiring from active management at age 46, he became an educator and philanthropist, writing bestselling books ('One Up on Wall Street', 'Beating the Street') that democratized investment knowledge. Lynch represents the antithesis of algorithmic investing: the belief that human judgment, curiosity, and discipline can outperform markets when applied rigorously.
